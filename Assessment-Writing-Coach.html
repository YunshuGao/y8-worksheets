Assessment vs Evaluation — What's the Difference?

Assessment

Helps you learn and improve.

Your teacher looks at your work to give you feedback so you can do better.

Evaluation

Decides how good your final work is.

Your teacher gives a grade or mark based on set criteria.

Key idea: Assessment helps you improve BEFORE the final grade. This writing coach is an assessment tool — it helps you improve your portfolio writing before your teacher evaluates it.

📝 KEY VERBS

Key Verbs — The Most Important Word in Any Question

The verb (action word) in a question tells you WHAT to do and HOW MUCH detail to include.

Identify / List
Name things. Short answers.
Least detail
State / Describe
Say what something is. Give details.
Some detail
Define / Explain
Give the meaning. Say WHY or HOW.
More detail
Evaluate / Justify
Judge quality. Give reasons for your opinion.
Most detail
Remember: Higher-level verbs (like explain and evaluate) need longer, more detailed answers. Lower-level verbs (like list) need shorter answers. The verb tells you how much to write!

ALARM & Breaking Down Questions

Learn to read questions carefully and know exactly what is being asked

About 25 minutes • Work through at your own pace

📖 LEARN

A.L.A.R.M. — A Learning and Responding Matrix

ALARM helps you understand HOW MUCH detail a question needs. Each level builds on the one before:

A — Acquire
identify, list, name, recall, label

Just remember and name things

L — Link
describe, state, define, outline

Connect ideas together, give details

A — Analyse
explain, compare, contrast, discuss

Break apart, find reasons and patterns

R — Respond
evaluate, justify, assess, recommend

Make judgements with evidence

M — Make
create, design, develop, construct

Build or produce something new

📖 LEARN

VSCC — Breaking Down a Question

Every assessment question has 4 parts. Finding them helps you know exactly what to write.

V — Verb

The action word. What do I need to DO?

S — Subject

The main topic. What is this about?

C — Content

The specific details. What information should I include?

C — Concept

The big idea. What is the deeper meaning?

Worked Example

Question: "Describe the need and benefits of upcycling shipping containers to create a portable container burger restaurant."

V — Describe

Give details about it — more than just a list

S — Need and benefits

Why we need this and what the advantages are

C — Upcycling shipping containers

Turning old containers into something new

C — Portable restaurant

A burger restaurant that can move to festivals

ALARM Level: Link (because the verb is "describe")

📖 EXAMPLES AT EACH LEVEL

See How Different Levels Look

E — Limited (1 mark)
"A shipping container can be a restaurant."
Only identifies what it is. No details or reasons.
D — Basic (2 marks)
"Shipping containers can be turned into restaurants. This is good because it reuses old containers."
States a benefit but does not give much detail.
C — Sound (3 marks)
"Upcycling shipping containers into burger restaurants is beneficial because it gives old containers a new purpose instead of sending them to landfill. The container provides a strong structural frame, which means less building materials are needed."
States benefits with some reasons. Uses linking words.
B — High (4 marks)
"There is a growing need for sustainable food outlets that can serve communities at festivals and events. Upcycling shipping containers addresses this need because it repurposes unused containers, reducing waste while creating a portable restaurant space. The modular design of containers makes them ideal because they can be transported by truck and set up quickly at different locations."
Defines the need and describes multiple benefits with detail.
A — Outstanding (5 marks)
"The growing demand for sustainable and portable food outlets has created a need for innovative restaurant solutions. Upcycling shipping containers addresses this need by repurposing the estimated 11 million unused containers worldwide, which would otherwise contribute to environmental waste. Furthermore, the structural strength of shipping containers means they require minimal additional building materials, making the process cost-effective. The standardised dimensions of containers also make them ideal for modular design, allowing restaurant owners to transport them easily to festivals and community events while maintaining food safety standards through proper ventilation and hygiene facilities."
Describes the need AND multiple benefits in detail, with evidence, linking words, and connection to the design brief.
